EFFORT BALANCE
The 80/20 rule is one of the most well-researched principles in endurance training. Scientists like Dr. Stephen Seiler found that elite runners do ~80% of their training at low intensity and only ~20% at moderate to high intensity. Doing too much hard training leads to injury and burnout. Your target: Easy ≥80%, Hard ≤20%.
MONTHLY VOLUME
Monthly volume tracks your total distance per month. Running coaches recommend increasing volume by no more than 10% per month to reduce injury risk — this is the "10% rule." Look for a gradual upward trend with occasional down-weeks for recovery. Beginners typically run 20–40km/month, recreational runners 40–80km, serious runners 80–150km+.
WEEKLY CONSISTENCY
Consistency is the #1 predictor of running improvement — more so than any single workout. Running 3–4 times per week for months beats occasional hard weeks. Benchmarks: Beginner = 2–3 runs/wk · Recreational = 3–4 · Intermediate = 4–5 · Advanced = 5–7. Missing more than one week in a row significantly increases injury risk when returning.
FITNESS (CTL)
CTL (Chronic Training Load) is a 42-day exponentially weighted average of your daily Training Stress Score. Formula: CTL_today = CTL_yesterday + (TSS_today − CTL_yesterday) / 42. A higher number means you've been consistently training hard over many weeks — it represents your aerobic fitness base. It takes 6–8 weeks to move by 10 points in either direction.
—
FATIGUE (ATL)
ATL (Acute Training Load) is a 7-day exponentially weighted average — the same formula as CTL but over 7 days instead of 42. It responds quickly to training spikes. After a hard week, ATL jumps up; after rest, it drops within days. A high ATL relative to CTL is normal during training blocks — it means you're pushing harder than your average.
—
FORM (TSB)
TSB (Training Stress Balance) = CTL − ATL. Simple subtraction, but powerful. It tells you whether your fitness currently exceeds your fatigue (positive = fresh) or vice versa (negative = fatigued). Most runners train at TSB −10 to −30. Racing or peaking should happen at TSB +5 to +25. Below −40 is a warning sign for overtraining.
—

Each pair of bars is one week. The teal bar is your CTL (fitness, slow-moving), the coral bar is your ATL (fatigue, fast-moving). When ATL spikes above CTL you're working harder than your base — this is normal and good in moderation. A widening gap between the two signals you may need a recovery week. Ideally CTL should trend upward over months.

RELATIVE EFFORT PER RUN (TRIMP)
TRIMP (Training Impulse) was developed by Dr. Eric Banister in 1991 and is the science behind Strava's "Relative Effort" and Apple's "Workout Effort." Formula: TRIMP = duration(min) × HR_reserve × 0.64 × e^(1.92 × HR_reserve). When heart rate isn't available we estimate from pace. Benchmarks per run: Easy recovery <30 · Easy aerobic 30–60 · Moderate 60–90 · Hard 90–120 · Very hard >120.
■ Easy ■ Moderate ■ Hard
PACE ZONES
Pace zones are calculated from your estimated threshold pace — the fastest pace you could hold for roughly 60 minutes. SoleSurvivor estimates this from your 10th-percentile fastest runs. Zone 1 (recovery) is >140% of threshold pace. Zone 2 (aerobic base) is 120–140%. Zone 3 (tempo) is 106–120%. Zone 4 (threshold) is 100–106%. Zone 5 (VO2 max) is <100%. Apple Watch uses heart rate to assign zones; we use pace as a proxy.
BEST EFFORTS
Best efforts are your fastest estimated times over standard race distances, extrapolated from your actual run paces. We find the run with the best average pace and project that over the target distance. This mirrors Strava's Best Efforts feature (which is behind their paywall) and is calculated from your free Strava data. As you log faster runs, these numbers update automatically.
PACE BENCHMARKS — WHERE DO YOU STAND?
These benchmarks are based on published finishing time distributions for recreational and competitive runners from race result databases and academic studies. "Beginner" = bottom 25%, "Recreational" = 25–60%, "Intermediate" = 60–80%, "Advanced" = 80–95%, "Elite" = top 5%. Age and gender affect these significantly — a 50-year-old recreational runner completing a 5K is performing at a very different physiological level than the raw time suggests. Your estimated VO2 max is calculated from your best mile pace using the Daniels/Gilbert formula.
